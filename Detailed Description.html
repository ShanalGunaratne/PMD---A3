Project In-Depth

Aims

The aim of our project is to create a fully functioning medical chatbot called PMD. The fully functioning PMD will be integrated with artificial intelligence and machine learning. This will allow it to constantly learn medical symptoms and speech to develop into a chatbot equivalent to a professional medical practitioner. Currently we have limited time, therefore we cannot integrate artificial intelligence or machine learning. However, we have created a few goals to show our project's potential and to reach our aim. The first goal is to create a prototype that will have the potential to diagnose 10 different illnesses. The second goal is to create a landing page to facilitate our chatbot. Third goal is to create graphical designs to enhance the user experience. Finally, create ads to market our chatbot. The above goals are listed according to priority.

The first goal is to create a prototype by using python. Visual studio will be used to code the chatbot, then we will create a separate discord server to facilitate and run the chatbot. Finally, users can access the service of our chatbot through the server and receive a diagnosis according to the information they communicate to the chatbot. This will be created to showcase the potential of a PMD and its usefulness to the general public.

The second goal is to create a landing page to make it more accessible to the public and any investors. This page will be a website that can be easily accessed by anyone with basic IT knowledge to diagnose themselves easily. Its main purpose is to provide accessibility to all users that need a quick diagnosis, with the minimum requirements possible.

The third goal is to create graphical designs to enhance and provide a more entertaining user experience. As it is a very mechanical process, unlike a visit to the doctor, it will need a more aesthetic view to create a more friendly interaction between the user and PMD. Another reason is to show uniqueness and set it apart from other chatbots.

Finally, our chatbot will need to be promoted to the public. For this we will create google advertisements that will be able to reach our target consumer market and provide them with the services of PMD. The advertisements might also help us to gain any interested individuals that might help us to reach our final aim.

Plans and Progress

The PMD chatbot, short for Pre-Medical Diagnosis chatbot, is designed to give users a chance to figure out what their medical diagnosis will be prior to attending an appointment with their General Practitioner (GP) - hence, the "Pre" part of the name.

Indeed, the idea for the PMD chatbot began with using IBM Watson to create an AI chatbot medical chatbot that could diagnose 30-40 illnesses, and this idea evolved into a discord python chatbot prototype due to our limited knowledge of how to create such an advanced chatbot and the limited timeframe in which we could create the PMD prototype.

This project is targeted towards a relatively young demographic, ranging from teenagers to young adults, and aims to spark interest in how a medical diagnosis works (eg. The chatbot conversation with the user mirrors how a GP would be asking about symptoms that the patient is experiencing in order to narrow down the potential disease that the patient is experiencing), and therefore, get young people intrigued about the field of medicine and becoming a doctor.

The PMD chatbot was created using the current version of Python (Python 3.10) and its libraries, as well as Microsoft Studio code editor, to edit the chatbot's code. Discord.py (the primary library for creating and modifying the PMD chatbot) and the extensions installed to help code the PMD chatbot easier included: the Material Icon Theme by Philip Kief, Python v2022.16.0 by Microsoft and Pylance v2022.10.10 by Microsoft.

Additionally, we used our knowledge of HTML, Bootstrap and CSS to create the PMD chatbot advertisement and information website - and; this website directed users to the discord server where the PMD chatbot was operating and also where users could read up on information about the PMD chatbot.

Furthermore, the testing for the PMD chatbot was done through three separate public chats on a discord server in order to determine if the chatbot was able to continue responding to test user inputs - the test users were the people in our group. Each time a medical diagnosis (in other words, a new physical illness was added to the chatbot) was added to the code of the PMD chatbot, we tested how the chatbot would respond to user inputs to prevent the risk of us missing any bugs, and to isolate where potential errors were coming from. Some of the issues that we encountered included: when the PMD chatbot was unable to connect to the discord server and therefore did not reply to the user input; and overloading potentially occurring if user commands were entered into the public chats simultaneously, as this would have caused the chatbot to simply shut down.

Risks

After discussing with a medical professional, a risk that was identified with this chatbot was the potential ethical issues with getting a diagnosis from a chatbot instead of a human doctor, as they felt that a doctor chatbot would be unable to replace the experience and knowledge of a human doctor.

The doctor also pointed out that a patient's medical history (e.g. allergies, asthma, family's medical history, etc.) is also important for diagnosis and that mental health issues also may influence the onset of the physical disease - and since we do not have a way of ensuring that the chatbot has a strong level of cybersecurity, we decided not to include the chatbot asking for and recording a patient's medical history for a diagnosis because we do not want to face the ethical and legal consequences if their personal data is leaked.

In addition, we also decided not to include the diagnosis of mental health issues because our chatbot would not be able to have the human empathy and experience to achieve it. Which would make it incapable of providing a mental health diagnosis to a patient and, therefore, may treat the patient insensitively.

For example, it was discovered in 2018 that the chatbot Woebot was incapable of ethically responding to reports of child sexual abuse because when BBC tried to test the Woebot by inputting the line "I'm being forced to have sex, and I'm only 12 years old", Woebot responded with "Sorry you're going through this, but it also shows me how much you care about connexion and that's really kind of beautiful." (White 2018).

Further, when BBC tried to test other therapist chatbots on how they would respond to reports of eating disorders, one of the chatbots, Wysa, said, "It's always nice to learn more about you and what makes you happy." "After the BBC tester responded "throwing up" to a previous Wysa question about what they were looking forward to for the day (White 2018). BBC also found that when the Woebot received the tester's input that they were smoking drugs and that they thought it was illegal but ultimately fine in the end, Wysa would respond, "Nice job!" (White 2018).

Hence, we did not include the chatbot asking about a patient's mental health and including that in the diagnosis of a physical illness, which meant that the chatbot was not capable of providing a full diagnosis of the physical illness, and what may have helped cause it - and also because the chatbot does not have a strong level of cybersecurity that would otherwise ensure that extremely sensitive medical data, such a patient's mental health issues, is not leaked.

Furthermore, while therapists' chatbots are currently being used, there is a scarcity of research on how effective they actually are - and the research that is present is done by the companies who created these chatbots in the first place, therefore resulting in the high risk of this research being potentially biased (Browne, 2022).

Additionally, since there is currently barely any government regulation over mental health apps, this means that current therapist chatbots can essentially mislead patients over how competent they will be in providing them mental health treatment - which could result in the patient fully trusting in a therapist chatbot, even if it gives them inadequate treatment, and therefore is another legal and ethical risk that we wanted to avoid with our chatbot (Browne, 2022).

lso, the researcher Estelle Smith (2022) discovered that the therapist chatbot Woebot would congratulate the user for "taking care of their mental and physical health" when she tested how it would respond to suicidality in a patient - which therefore puts into question the competency of therapist chatbots pertaining to their ability to treat patients (Browne, 2022) effectively.

In addition, an NBCI review (2020) says that while chatbots do "have the potential to improve mental health", the research on this matter has contradictory results, and the Texas University professor of medical humanities and philosophy Tekin (n.d.) says that therapist chatbot "creat[e] the illusion of help" all point to the current technology available not being sophisticated enough to make proper therapist chatbots, we decided not to include a therapy element with our chatbot, and therefore not risk a patient's mental health with a medical chatbot unable to give them sufficient treatment (Browne, 2022).

Furthermore, including mental health diagnosis as a part of the medical chatbot means that an age limit would need to be imposed on the users of the medical chatbot, which renders the main purpose of this chatbot (being usable for kids, and gaining their interest in a simplified representation of how medical diagnosis works, and therefore gain their interest in medicine) useless because then the chatbot would be primarily used by adults.

Additionally, some symptoms are visual (eg. extreme redness in the face or any other body part). Since the chatbot does not have eyes, it cannot see what the patient's visual symptoms look like (as a patient may overlook some visual symptoms) because chatbots cannot yet make use of the camera on the device that the chatbot is being used on -. In contrast, human doctors are able to see any visible symptoms of certain illnesses that the patient may have overlooked or dismissed as unimportant. Also, even if chatbots did become sophisticated enough to be able to access the camera on the device (that the person is using the chatbot on), the potentially poor lighting conditions or quality of the camera could affect how clear the picture is, and therefore how accurately the chatbot would be able to diagnose the patient off of the visual symptom shown in the photo.

Ultimately, to avoid further ethical and legal consequences, we decided to circumvent this risk by including disclaimers that the PMD chatbot is not a replacement for an actual human doctor because it cannot diagnose mental health issues or ask about the patient's medical history, or provide prescribed medicine for the patient to use, this means that the PMD chatbot cannot provide a full diagnosis and therefore proper treatment for the patient - and that after every diagnosis that chatbot gives a patient, there will be a line stating that they should still go and see their GP afterwards because the medical chatbot is not a replacement for the experience, knowledge and empathy of a human doctor. This would therefore prevent the risk of the patient being given misleading information about the actual competency of the PMD chatbot about how it can replace it.

Moreover, another function of our chatbot is that it can help patients find a GP that is close to them by providing a link to another website (not made by us) that helps the patient find a GP by typing in their location. Additionally, the chatbot can also help a patient arrange an appointment with a GP by including a link to another website (not made by us) that helps a patient arrange an appointment with a GP. This, therefore, prevents the risk of their personal data (e.g. their home address and medical history) being stolen or leaked, as the chatbot would not be asking after and recording such sensitive information.

Another major risk and concern is user privacy. A doctor's visit is very personal as it involves communicating about issues that an individual will not feel comfortable sharing with others. Therefore, choosing to share with an artificial entity will pose another level of difficulty. However, with the development and evolution of technology, the younger generation is more comfortable communicating with chatbots. Unfortunately, with the development of tech, the higher risk of cybercrimes. Losing user privacy is a major concern and risk to PMD. In order to reduce the risk of cybercrimes, constant upgrades and checks of cybersecurity software will need to be conducted.
